Cognition and Brain Circuitry Laboratory
Principle Investigator: David Osher, Ph.D.

David Osher

Principle Investigator, Assistant Professor of Psychology
Ph.D., Computational Neuroscience, Massachusetts Institute of Technology

My research explores the relationship between brain connectivity, neural function, and behavior, through computational modeling. Connectivity is the primary constraint on the information available to a brain region, and so connectivity should be highly predictive of neural responses. I am primarily interested in attention and high-level visual perception, and I have demonstrated that structure-function models can predict how your brain will respond during a task, before you even do the task, using your connectivity patterns alone. These models can also predict a variety of cognitive abilities at the level of an individual subject.

Fiona Molloy

Graduate Student
B.S., Neuroscience, The Ohio State University

I’m a third year graduate student pursuing a PhD in Cognitive Neuroscience and Master of Applied Statistics, co-advised by Dr. David Osher and Dr. Zeynep Saygin. I received a BS in neuroscience from OSU in 2018. I am interested in using computational modeling and neuroimaging to understand how individual differences in the brain can shape behavior and change from infancy to adulthood. I am particularly interested in the neural mechanisms underlying habituation and cognitive control. Currently, I am working with Dr. Osher to find the underlying structure and organization of functional networks in the brain, while accounting for individual variability in the mapping of these functional networks.
